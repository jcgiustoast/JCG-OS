# LTV Frameworks

> IF YOU HAVE THE VOLUME, EXPERIMENT EVERY CHANGE YOU INTRODUCE.

## The Problem with Traditional LTV

The heuristic "if your LTV:CAC ratio > 3, you're good" is borrowed from VC-backed SaaS with minimal variable costs. It's dangerous for eCommerce because:

- Self-funded eCommerce brands cannot wait years for CAC payback
- Unlike SaaS, eCommerce requires capital for production, storage, shipping
- Following the 3:1 ratio creates cash flow problems that can kill the business

**Example:** A brand acquiring customers for $15, with first-order value of $20 (costing $10 to produce) and LTV of $45 (taking one year to realize) faces potential bankruptcy without adequate capital reserves.

## The Refined LTV Framework

Two essential corrections to traditional LTV:

### 1. Value, Not Revenue

**VALUE = REVENUE - VARIABLE COSTS (COGS + SHIPPING)**

A customer generating $100 in annual revenue with 50% variable costs represents a $50 LTV, not $100.

This distinction provides:
- Actual visibility into profitability and cash position
- Actionable metrics teams can test against
- True economic picture of customer worth

### 2. Time-Bounded Metrics

Replace single LTV number with time-specific windows:

| Window | Use Case |
|--------|----------|
| **60-Day LTV** | Short-term acquisition decisions, ad spend calibration |
| **120-Day LTV** | Medium-term retention evaluation |
| **1-Year LTV** | Long-term business health |
| **1+ Year LTV** | Strategic planning |

This enables shorter decision cycles and more actionable insights.

## The 60-Day LTV Multiplier

From the [[ecommerce-growth-formula]]:

**60-Day LTV Multiplier** = how much a cohort spends within 60 days post-first-purchase, compared to initial AOV.

**Example:** A cohort acquired in August with $50 AOV reaching $75 average LTV by October = 1.5x multiplier (50% additional value).

This balances SaaS-style LTV thinking with eCommerce cash flow reality. Different industries have naturally varying multipliers (coffee vs jewelry), but improvement is achievable across sectors.

## 7 Strategies to Improve LTV

1. **Subscription offerings** -- create predictable recurring revenue
2. **Post-purchase sequences** -- capitalize on Month 0 momentum through email/SMS
3. **Strategic upselling** -- leverage checkout pages, account dashboards, order tracking pages
4. **Timely customer flows** -- target repurchase windows identified through purchase pattern analysis
5. **Multi-channel communication** -- test SMS, push notifications, communities, apps with measured incremental impact
6. **Unboxing experiences** -- strengthen brand perception and retention
7. **Loyalty programs** -- membership and affiliate initiatives encourage repeat engagement

## Subscription-Specific LTV Considerations

From [[subscription-metrics]]:
- Subscribers generate 2.5-4x more revenue than one-time purchasers
- Prepaid subscribers show 30-50% better retention than monthly
- A 5% increase in subscription take rate = 15-25% more LTV
- Customers rarely upgrade before fourth renewal (retention enables expansion)

### The 60-Day Evaluation Rule

Subscription experiments MUST be evaluated on 60-day LTV, not immediate conversion/AOV. A variant that drops conversion 1.25% but increases 60-day LTV by 7% is a winner -- invisible under standard testing methodology.

See [[experimentation-frameworks]] for how to set up subscription cohort tracking.

## LTV by Customer Segment

From the Awake Customers framework (see [[profitability-levers]]):
- **Active Recurring** -- highest repurchase probability, most valuable
- **At-Risk Recurring** -- declining probability, still reachable
- **Dormant Recurring** -- low probability, expensive to reactivate

Time cutoffs vary by industry. Jewelry: 100 days might be "Active." Supplements: 100 days is likely "Dormant."

Cohort analysis in Shopify reveals that regardless of entry month, the LTV increase curve is remarkably consistent -- making recurring customer revenue highly forecastable. See [[ecommerce-forecasting]].
