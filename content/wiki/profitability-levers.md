# Profitability Levers

> Growth at any cost is dead. Those who continue chasing ROAS end up, sooner or later, hitting the wall.

## The Worst Mistake: Optimizing Revenue Instead of Profit

Common scenario in 7-figure eCommerces:
- CMO: "Revenue target achieved at 110%"
- Agency: "Achieved 7x ROAS"
- CFO: "Profitability dropped 20%"

The root cause: teams optimize for the wrong metric. Revenue sits far from actual profit in the P&L. Obsessing over it leads to unprofitable ad investments, margin-destroying promotions, customer fatigue, and celebrating sales records while finances deteriorate.

## Marginal Contribution: The North Star

**Marginal Contribution = NET REVENUE - DELIVERY COST - ADVERTISING INVESTMENT**

Where:
- **Net Revenue** = Total Revenue - VAT - Returns
- **Delivery Cost** = Product + Shipping + Payment Commission (all variable costs)
- **Advertising Investment** = Performance + brand advertising, prorated across channels

This remainder covers: fixed expenses, growth investment, dividend distribution.

### Why It Beats Alternatives

1. **Closest metric to actual profit** that marketing teams can control
2. **Calculable daily** (unlike monthly net profit)
3. **Directly influenced** by marketing decisions

### Reverse Engineering Approach

Start with Marginal Contribution objectives and work backward to calculate necessary revenue, ad spend, and non-negotiable margins. This inverts traditional revenue-chasing logic.

## The 11 Profitability Levers

From auditing P&Ls of companies between 1-10M EUR annually. These can unlock 15-40% additional profitability without touching acquisition budget.

### 1. Price Testing
90% of audited eCommerces have never systematically tested prices. In a recent client, an 8% price increase maintained conversion intact and added 180K EUR annually directly to EBITDA. Understanding price elasticity is extraordinarily powerful.

### 2. Bid Controls
Without strict CPA or minimum ROAS objectives, you're telling Facebook/Google: "keep spending regardless of profitability." Platforms optimize for their billing, not your profit. Ensure the bottleneck is in volume, not efficiency.

### 3. Supplier Contract Renegotiation
Sales volume changes but supplier contracts don't. 5-12% reductions in variable costs (COGS, logistics, software, commissions) are achievable simply by renegotiating based on current volumes.

### 4. Incrementality Tests
Platform ROAS overestimates real impact by 20-60%. Brand search campaigns on Google exaggerate results up to 5x. If you invest more than 10K EUR monthly in digital, incrementality tests are mandatory. See [[measurement-incrementality]].

### 5. Product Mix Optimization
Not all products are equal. Consider: demand levels, acquisition costs, gross margin, LTV generation, inventory age. Prioritize products that maximize total contribution and cash flow, not just sales volume.

### 6. Return Rate Improvement
Returns are a silent margin killer. A 5% reduction in returns typically equals 8-12% increase in net margin. Implement questionnaires, study responses, improve product quality, add AI size guides or instruction manuals.

### 7. Recurring Revenue Development
The Holy Grail isn't the first sale but the following ones. Subscriptions or membership models create predictable income and reduced acquisition costs. If your product isn't recurring, create memberships with exclusive discounts (like True Classic).

### 8. Marketing Operations Automation
AI-powered creation of copy, creatives, web pages, videos. Tool audit: typical savings of 2-5K EUR monthly in unnecessary subscriptions.

### 9. Demand-Generating Marketing Moments
Not just generic evergreen ads -- give customers a reason to buy today. Leverage seasonal trends, industry events, high-intent moments. If you sell children's products: back-to-school. Men's products: Movember.

### 10. New Market Segment Diversification
Sometimes more creatives aren't enough. Examples: Blue Banana launched Athletics collection for the sports market. RIDGE constantly releases new wallet colors + develops powerbanks, rings, suitcases.

### 11. Eliminate Destructive Discounts (Bonus)
Constant discounts train customers to wait for promotions. Byron Sharp documented in "How Brands Grow" that much of discount-driven sales are non-incremental. Use discounts sporadically for maximum impact.

## The 7 Habits of Highly Profitable eCom Brands

1. **Profitability is the North Star, not revenue** -- every decision evaluated on marginal contribution impact
2. **Absolute control of bidding and ad efficiency** -- defined minimum CPA/ROAS targets per campaign
3. **They test incrementality, not platform ROAS** -- systematic testing to find genuine additional sales
4. **They analyze and optimize product mix** -- continuous evaluation of margins, acquisition costs, LTV by product
5. **They don't abuse discounts** -- strategic deployment, not indiscriminate promotional activity
6. **Radical efficiency in fixed costs and operations** -- audit subscriptions, renegotiate contracts, AI automation
7. **Daily tracking with unified marginal contribution dashboard** -- real-time course corrections, not month-end surprises

## The Growth Trap: Cutting Ads to Boost Margins

A cautionary framework from the "Why Your eCommerce Stopped Growing" article:

**The Tank Analogy:** Recurring customers are like fuel in a tank. For long trips (medium/long-term growth), you must "refuel" through acquisition investment. If you floor the accelerator without refueling (cut ads while squeezing existing customers), the tank empties.

### The Awake Customers Framework

**Awake Customers = New Customers Acquired + Active Recurring + At-Risk Recurring - Dormant Recurring**

Not all recurring customers are equal:
- **Active Recurring** — purchased recently
- **At-Risk Recurring** — purchased some time ago, not yet considered lost
- **Dormant Recurring** — haven't purchased in a long time, unlikely to return

Time cutoffs vary by industry (jewelry: 100 days might be "Active"; supplements: 100 days is "Dormant").

**Case study:** A $20M/year client cut ad spend 50% to boost contribution margin 30%. Six months later, revenue had dropped 20%. They had cut Awake Customers in half. Recovery required lowering growth expectations, gradually rebuilding ad spend, and 6 months to get back on track.
