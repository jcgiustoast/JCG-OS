# A New Way of Thinking About LTV in Ecommerce

## The Problem with Our Current Understanding of LTV

The business world relies on many rules of thumb, but some prove misleading. One popular heuristic suggests that "if your LTV to CAC ratio is bigger than 3, you are good to go." This framework may work for VC-backed SaaS companies with minimal variable costs, but it creates serious problems for ecommerce businesses.

Most ecommerce brands face challenges that SaaS companies don't:

* Self-founded operations cannot wait extended periods for customer repayment
* Ecommerce requires capital for production, storage, and shipping -- unlike SaaS with near-zero marginal costs

Following the 3:1 ratio blindly creates "a cashflow-negative business" heading toward bankruptcy. Consider this example: acquiring customers for $15, with first-order AOV of $20 (costing $10 to produce) and one-year LTV of $45 means early losses that strain cash reserves.

## A New Way of Thinking About LTV

A better approach requires three characteristics: accounting for variable costs, using specific time periods, and remaining actionable.

### Considering Variable Costs

The distinction between revenue and value matters in ecommerce:

* Revenue = total earned amount
* Value = "Revenue - Variable Costs (Cost of Goods Sold + Shipping)"

A customer generating $100 in annual revenue with 50% variable costs produces $50 LTV under this framework.

### Considering Time Periods

Annual measurements lack actionability. Adding time-period components creates measurable milestones:

* **60-Day LTV**: profit within 60 days post-acquisition
* **120-Day LTV**: profit within 120 days post-acquisition
* **1-Year LTV**: profit within 365 days post-acquisition
* **1+-Year LTV**: profit within 2-3 years post-acquisition

This approach enables strategy testing and improvement tracking without waiting years for results.

### Benefits of This New LTV Paradigm

This refined thinking provides several advantages:

**Actionable**: Test improvements directly rather than relying on proxy metrics like email open rates.

**Flexible**: Time periods adapt to your business -- use 90, 120, or 169-day windows as needed.

**Closer to Cash**: Subtracting variable costs reveals actual profitability, supporting better business decisions.

Understanding 60-day and 1-year LTV enables brands to "aim for breakeven on first order" while ensuring adequate cash flow sustainability. Detecting poor 60-day LTV signals either a one-time purchase business or a significant growth opportunity.

## 5 Ways to Improve LTV

### Offering Subscriptions

Subscriptions represent "the holy grail of LTV improvement" for products with recurring utility. They guarantee repeat purchases without ongoing acquisition effort, though aggressive promotion risks credibility damage.

### Optimizing Post-Purchase Flows

Customer purchasing peaks during month zero -- the acquisition month itself. Yet many brands neglect post-purchase email and SMS flows, missing powerful engagement tools during peak receptivity windows.

### Upselling and Cross-Selling

Beyond typical product recommendations, leverage post-purchase touchpoints including thank-you pages, account pages, and order tracking interfaces to suggest complementary products based on purchase history.

### Creating New Flows

Identify average repurchase timing patterns. If customers typically return 75 days post-purchase, design sequences that "make them fall in love with your brand again" at optimal moments.

### Introducing New Communication Channels

Expand beyond email to test SMS, push notifications, Facebook communities, and apps. Measure incremental impact rather than attributed revenue, since channel costs may exceed benefits.

### Improve the Unboxing Experience

First physical product interaction shapes brand perception significantly. Quality unboxing experiences improve retention rates and customer lifetime value.

### Developing Membership or Affiliate Programs

Membership and affiliate programs encourage repeat engagement. Numerous apps facilitate implementation, though rigorous testing ensures incremental revenue generation.

## Conclusion

The fundamental principle underlying LTV optimization remains: "IF YOU HAVE THE VOLUME, EXPERIMENT EVERY CHANGE YOU INTRODUCE."

A/B testing reveals impact clarity and enables retention of only positive changes. Without sufficient volume, you risk implementing changes with unknown consequences.
