# 10 eCommerce Growth and Finance Metrics You Should Know

**Author:** Juan Cruz Giusto
**Date:** July 17, 2025

---

## Introduction

Traditional metrics like ROAS, gross revenue, and conventional AOV offer an incomplete view of the business. While the competition chases a potentially inflated 4x ROAS, there are more precise metrics that reveal the financial truth and enable impactful decisions to grow profitably in 2025.

---

## 1. Contribution Margin

**Formula:** Net Revenue - Variable Costs - Advertising Cost

**Components:**
- Net Revenue: Total Revenue - Returns - VAT
- Variable Costs: COGS + Shipping + Platform Commissions
- Advertising Cost: Ad spend attributed to sales

This metric represents what actually remains to cover fixed costs, invest in growth, and distribute dividends. It is superior to revenue because it identifies businesses that generate millions in revenue but have negative contribution margin.

---

## 2. Incrementality Factor

**Formula:** Real Results / Platform-Reported Results

Ad platforms tend to inflate results by attributing sales that would have occurred naturally.

**Example:** An incrementality test pausing Madrid (EUR 20K investment) reduces revenue from EUR 100K to EUR 60K. Meta reported EUR 50K in impact, but the real impact was EUR 40K. Incrementality factor: 0.8 (80%).

**Application:** Multiply the platform ROAS by the incrementality factor to obtain the real ROAS and compensate for inflated attribution.

---

## 3. Payback Period

The number of days it takes to recover the acquisition cost through customer revenue.

**Formula:** Payback Period = nCAC / (AOV x Gross Margin x Monthly Purchase Frequency) x 30 days

**Example:**
- nCAC: EUR 60
- AOV: EUR 100
- Gross Margin: 40%
- Purchase Frequency: 0.8 times/month
- **Result: 56 days**

Critical because it indicates whether there is enough cash flow to sustain growth without external financing. A 6-month payback period with 3 months of cash flow is problematic.

---

## 4. Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)

The time difference between paying for inventory and receiving money from its sale.

**Formula:** CCC = DIO + DSO - DPO

**Components:**
- **DIO** (Days Inventory Outstanding): (Average Inventory / COGS) x 365
- **DSO** (Days Sales Outstanding): (Accounts Receivable / Sales) x 365
- **DPO** (Days Payable Outstanding): (Accounts Payable / COGS) x 365

**Example:** 45 + 2 - 60 = **-13 days**

A negative CCC is ideal because it allows you to collect before paying, generating positive cash flow that can be reinvested immediately.

---

## 5. Average Gross Margin per User

**Formula:** Conversion Rate x AOV x Average Gross Margin

The average gross margin generated by each website user. Superior to ARPU because it considers variable costs in addition to revenue.

**Example:** Two eCommerce businesses with AOV EUR 65: the first converts 2% with 30% margin, the second converts 2% with 40% margin. Same revenue, completely different profitability.

---

## 6. Gross AOV / nCAC

Measures the average gross margin of an order versus the acquisition cost of new customers.

**Components:**
- Gross Margin = AOV - Variable Costs (COGS + Shipping + Commissions)
- nCAC = Ad Spend / New Customer Orders

**Example:**
- First-order AOV: EUR 100, Variable costs: EUR 55
- First-order gross margin: EUR 45
- Ad spend: EUR 10,000, New orders: 200
- nCAC: EUR 50
- **Ratio: EUR 45 / EUR 50 = 0.9**

A result below 1 means a loss per customer on the first order. It requires that LTV at 60-90 days compensates, or the ratio must be improved.

---

## 7. aMER (Acquisition Marketing Efficiency Ratio)

**Formula:** New Customer Revenue / Ad Spend

Critical difference: the traditional MER includes recurring sales (reactivatable via email, SMS). The aMER focuses only on new customers, revealing the real efficiency of advertising.

Common reality: eCommerce businesses with a MER of 4x have an aMER of 2x because recurring sales are incorrectly attributed to advertising.

---

## 8. 60-Day LTV Multiplier

Estimates the total value a customer contributes in the first 60 days since acquisition. 60 days is chosen as the optimal balance between practicality and precision.

**Benchmark:** A good 60-day LTV would be around 15-30% of the total projected LTV, depending on the business.

Application: A low 60-day LTV indicates an enormous growth lever in post-purchase flows, upsells, and early retention.

---

## 9. 1-Year LTV Multiplier

The total value a customer contributes in the first year since acquisition.

**Benchmark:** A good 360-day LTV would be around 75-100% of the total projected LTV (except for products with very high margins).

Insight: A low 1-year LTV compared to benchmarks indicates a long-term retention problem that is not visible in short-term metrics.

---

## 10. Repeat Rate

The percentage of revenue from recurring customers versus new customers.

**Common trap:** A very high rate may indicate excessive dependence on recurring customers, harming long-term growth.

**Warning sign:** More than 60% of revenue from recurring customers may indicate piggy-bank depletion and advancing of natural sales.

---

## Conclusion

These superior metrics enable you to:

- Decide based on real profitability, not platform illusions
- Identify bottlenecks before they destroy quarters
- Optimize sustainable growth versus artificial results
- Protect cash flow while scaling
- Detect dangerous dependency on recurring customers

"What is not measured is not managed." When teams see and pursue these metrics daily, decisions automatically orient toward real profitability.
