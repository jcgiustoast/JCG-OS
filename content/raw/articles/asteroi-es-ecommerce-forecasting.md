# How to Forecast Your eCommerce Based on Data

**Author:** Juan Cruz Giusto
**Date:** October 4, 2025

---

## Introduction

> "There is no favorable wind for the one who does not know where they are going" -- Seneca

The forecast is an essential tool in eCommerce, but most do it without solid foundations, treating it as guessing instead of basing it on historical data and strategy. The common problem is that growing 30% requires substantial changes, not just repeating the previous year.

In many cases, forecasts end up as forgotten documents in Drive folders, reviewed barely once a month.

## How the Best Use the Forecast

Successful eCommerce businesses use this tool as a north star that sets objectives and a yardstick to measure results. The forecast becomes an execution exercise that serves to:

* Define realistic objectives based on historical data
* Set ambitious goals that motivate the team
* Create justifiable strategies for growth
* Work daily to meet objectives
* Quickly detect deviations
* Act to correct course

Although it may seem theoretical, it is essential for your eCommerce to scale profitably.

---

## The Tree Model: Understanding Your eCommerce

To forecast correctly, it is important to understand the parts that make up the business using the analogy of a tree with three fundamental parts:

### The Roots: Recurring Customers

They are the most profitable because they do not require re-acquisition. They are also the easiest to forecast because they tend to behave consistently.

### The Trunk: Organic New Customers

People who already know the brand but have not purchased. This includes social media followers, email/SMS contacts, or those who have interacted with posts. **These would buy without paid advertising.**

### The Branches: Paid New Customers

Unknown people who require convincing through advertising. They are the most expensive to acquire and hardest to forecast, depending on external platforms. **Without advertising, they would not buy.**

## Why Does This Analogy Matter?

A tree without deep roots falls with the first strong wind. An eCommerce that relies primarily on paid advertising to grow puts its business at risk in the face of changes such as:

* Changes in advertising rates
* Algorithm changes and restrictions (GDPR, iOS)
* New competition with low prices

It is normal that at the beginning most sales come from customers acquired through advertising. But as the eCommerce matures, it is crucial to increase the percentage of recurring customers and build brand to attract sales without depending solely on paid advertising.

## How to Forecast Each Part

### The Roots: Recurring Customers

They are the easiest to forecast because they tend to behave consistently. You can verify this in Shopify through cohort analysis: regardless of the entry month, the LTV increase is very similar.

**Keep in mind that this behavior may vary if:**

* You add new products to the catalog
* You improve SMS/email campaigns
* You introduce subscriptions or memberships

### How to Calculate It:

**Month 0:** The only recurring buyers will be those acquired in the past (Past Customers). Recurring customers will come from Month 1 of these past customers.

**Month 1:** Sum of:
* Month 2 of "Past Customers"
* Month 1 of customers acquired in Month 0

**Month 2:** Sum of:
* Month 3 of "Past Customers"
* Month 2 of Month 0 cohort
* Month 1 of Month 1 cohort

And so on.

**Key point:** The number of recurring customers depends on the number of customers acquired in past months. That is why it is important to forecast both recurring and new customers simultaneously.

### The Exterior: New Customers (Organic and Paid)

Although conceptually they are divided to explain their importance, in practice it is nearly impossible to make a precise distinction because it is difficult to know the true incrementality of marketing efforts.

With incrementality tests and MMM you can get a more precise estimate, but knowing it with certainty is impossible.

### The Practical Solution:

1. Analyze how many customers each channel has brought historically
2. Apply incrementality factors

### Practical Example:

Suppose an eCommerce that generated EUR 500,000 from new customers in October, spending EUR 100,000 on advertising: EUR 30K on Google and EUR 70K on Meta.

| Channel | Spend | Platform Revenue | Incrementality Factor | Estimated Real Revenue |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Google Ads Brand** | EUR 30,000 | EUR 160,000 | 0.3 | EUR 48,000 |
| **Meta Ads** | EUR 70,000 | EUR 250,000 | 1.2 | EUR 300,000 |

Applying the Incrementality Factor:

* **Real paid new customers** = EUR 48,000 (Google) + EUR 300,000 (Meta) = EUR 348,000
* **Organic new customers** = EUR 152,000 (difference between total new customer sales and what was generated by advertising)

With this data, you can forecast:

* **New customers from advertising:** Depending on how much you spend
* **Organic customers:** Base that does not change, plus another variable depending on ad investment (more people buying, more word of mouth)

### Important Considerations When Forecasting New Customers:

1. **Performance is based on historical data** and assumes everything stays the same. Improvements in creatives, conversion, or advertising incrementality will increase ROAS.

2. **Efficiency decreases with investment.** You cannot assume ROAS will remain the same if you double investment.

3. **Validate your incrementality factors** with real tests. If you have not run tests, you can rely on benchmarks.

4. **Consider seasonality.** Some months have greater acquisition capacity depending on the industry.

---

## Conclusion: The Forecast as an Execution Exercise

The forecast is not a guessing exercise where marketing and finance speculate about the future.

It must be a **strategy and execution exercise**, where objectives are defined based on the past and on what will be done differently to achieve them.

It is essential to:

* Understand the parts that make up your eCommerce
* Forecast each one
* Create strategies that move the needle on recurring customer retention and new customer acquisition (organic and paid)
