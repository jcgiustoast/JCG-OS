# A New Way of Thinking About LTV in Ecommerce

## The Problem with Our Current Understanding of LTV

The business world relies on various heuristics, some helpful and others misleading. One problematic rule of thumb suggests that "if your LTV to CAC ratio is bigger than 3, you are good to go." While this metric works for venture-backed SaaS companies with minimal variable costs, it proves problematic for most eCommerce businesses.

Self-funded eCommerce brands cannot afford to wait lengthy periods for customer acquisition costs to be recouped. Unlike SaaS with negligible marginal expenses, eCommerce requires capital for production, storage, and shipping. Following the traditional 3:1 ratio creates cash flow problems that can threaten business viability.

The article illustrates this danger with an example: a brand acquiring customers for $15, with a first-order value of $20 (costing $10 to produce) and an LTV of $45 (taking one year to realize), faces potential bankruptcy without adequate capital reserves.

## A Refined LTV Framework

The proposed approach incorporates three essential elements:

**Variable Costs Matter**: The new framework distinguishes between revenue (total earnings) and value (revenue minus variable costs including COGS and shipping). For instance, a customer generating $100 in annual revenue with 50% variable costs represents a $50 LTV.

**Time Periods Require Specificity**: Rather than relying solely on annual measures, the framework introduces time-bound metrics:
- 60-Day LTV
- 120-Day LTV
- 1-Year LTV
- 1+-Year LTV

This approach enables shorter decision cycles and more actionable business insights.

## Strategic Benefits

This refined methodology offers meaningful advantages:

- **Actionability**: Teams can test improvements directly against measurable outcomes rather than proxy metrics
- **Flexibility**: Timeframes adapt to individual business needs
- **Clarity**: Subtracting variable costs provides visibility into actual profitability and cash position

## Five Improvement Strategies

1. **Subscription offerings** create predictable recurring revenue
2. **Post-purchase sequences** capitalize on Month 0 momentum through email and SMS
3. **Strategic upselling** leverages checkout pages, account dashboards, and order tracking pages
4. **Timely customer flows** target repurchase windows identified through purchase pattern analysis
5. **Multi-channel communication** tests SMS, push notifications, communities, and apps with measured incremental impact
6. **Unboxing experiences** strengthen brand perception and retention
7. **Loyalty programs** including membership and affiliate initiatives encourage repeat engagement

## Final Recommendation

The article emphasizes a critical principle: "IF YOU HAVE THE VOLUME, EXPERIMENT EVERY CHANGE YOU INTRODUCE." A/B testing validates each strategic change, preventing implementation of initiatives that could harm long-term business performance.
