**CEO:** Let's bump the Google Brand PMax budget. The ROAS is incredible.

**CMO:** Correction — the *reported* ROAS is incredible. The reality is completely different.

**CEO:** How can that be? The Google dashboard clearly says ROAS 8.

**CMO:** We just got the incrementality test results on that campaign. What looked like a ROAS of 8 is really a 2.

**CEO:** Wait. We're only profitable above 3. You're telling me we're losing money on every sale?

**CMO:** Yes.

**CEO:** How? You're telling me Google is lying?

**CMO:** Yes and no. Google attributes sales based on impressions and clicks. If someone sees the ad and buys that day, Google claims it drove the sale.

**CEO:** And that's true, isn't it?

**CMO:** That part is true. But a lot of those people would have bought anyway. Google just showed up near the bottom of the funnel and took credit.

**CEO:** Got it. Someone seeing an ad doesn't mean the ad drove the purchase.

**CMO:** Exactly. Maybe that person saw an Instagram ad earlier — *that* was the real driver.

**CEO:** So Meta and Google both claim the same sale, even though Meta actually generated it.

**CMO:** That's why we can't trust what platforms tell us. You love football — think of it this way:

Judging a marketing channel by reported conversions is like judging a player only by their goals. Plenty of matches Messi didn't score in, and he was still 90% responsible for every goal.

**CEO:** And the only way to know who really drove what is incrementality testing.

**CMO:** You got it.

**CEO:** Set up incrementality tests for every channel where we spend seriously. And from now on, whenever we report platform ROAS, I want an incrementality factor included.

**CMO:** On it.
