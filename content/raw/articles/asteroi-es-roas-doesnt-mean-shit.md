# Your ROAS Doesn't Mean Shit

**By Juan Cruz Giusto**
June 25, 2025

---

## Introduction

And there are 3 specific reasons:

* Your ROAS lies and is easily manipulated
* It doesn't reflect the economic reality of your business
* It's only one piece of a much larger puzzle

In this post I explain why this metric is leading you down the wrong path and what you should measure instead.

## 1. Your ROAS Lies and Is Easily Manipulated

We've always had the intuition that ROAS isn't 100% reliable.

After all, the ultimate goal of advertising platforms (Meta, Google, etc.) is to generate value for their investors, and their business model depends on you spending more and more on advertising.

The way they achieve this is by inflating ROAS without you noticing.

In other words, they say they're generating more money than they're actually producing.

**Meta, for example:**

* Its default attribution window is 7-Day Click / 1-Day View
* It doesn't encourage you to exclude recent buyers
* The setting to change this is hidden in advanced configurations

**Google is even more aggressive:**

* Standard attribution window of 30 days post-click
* Brand campaigns are promoted as "high performance" when they're the least incremental

Imagine: you searched for your brand, bought 3 weeks later, and Google attributes that sale as a "paid conversion."

**The result:** Without constant incrementality tests, you could be overestimating your real ROI by 150-300%. Each platform has different incrementality, each campaign within a platform does too, and even the same campaign varies depending on creatives and configurations.

The only way to normalize ROAS is by understanding the incrementality factor of each strategy and knowing the iROAS of each one.

**If you invest more than $10K/month, implement incrementality tests.**

If you invest less, make sure to configure your campaigns as incrementally as possible:

* Exclude recent buyers
* Use tighter attribution windows (7 or 1 day click)
* Use more top-of-funnel creatives to reach people who don't yet know your solution

## 2. ROAS Ignores the Economic Reality of Your Business

Even if your ROAS were 100% accurate, it's still an incomplete metric, especially in eCommerce.

Here's a real example:

Your agency celebrates a ROAS of 3.0 (you spend $33 for every $100 in revenue).

But **the math reflects a not-so-optimistic reality**:

* Revenue: $100
* Acquisition cost: $33
* VAT (10%): -$10
* Product cost (30%): -$30
* Shipping (5%): -$5
* Returns (10%): -$10
* Payment processing (5%): -$5
* **Real margin: $7**

That ROAS 3.0 is actually a **real ROAS of 1.33**.

Doesn't sound so bad, until you remember that there are also fixed costs you need to cover.

Goodbye profit.

Not very fun, right?

And to complicate matters, **each product has different variable costs** (production costs, shipping, return rates, etc.) so the real ROAS calculation varies.

And all of this assuming the ROAS reported by the ad platform is truthful and not inflated, which is usually not the case.

## 3. It's Only One Piece of a Much Larger Puzzle

This is where most people make the fatal error: optimizing an isolated metric in a complex ecosystem.

**In your executive dashboard, in addition to ROAS you should have:**

* **Paid MER**: Total platform revenue _(ideally normalized by incrementality)_ / Ad spend

This metric will tell you the return on ad spend across all your sales channels.

* **aMER**: New customer revenue / Ad spend

This will help you understand the effectiveness of your advertising effort in acquiring new customers, also considering organic revenue from new customers.

* **Total MER**: Total revenue / Ad spend

This will help you understand the impact of all your marketing within your eCommerce, considering both new and recurring customers.

And beyond ratios, we have:

* Revenue generated by new customers
* Revenue generated by recurring customers
* LTV at 30/60/90/360 days for new customers
* Revenue generated by organic customers
* Contribution Margin generated by new customers
* Total Contribution Margin generated

**Looking only at ROAS means not understanding the full picture and can lead you to make wrong decisions**, over-optimizing this metric at the expense of others or of long-term growth.

For example, if a new customer's LTV increases 70% at 90 days, it may make sense to target a low ROAS (even breakeven) because you know the investment recovers quickly. But if you only look at ROAS, you'd never discover this.

That's why **a comprehensive strategy that considers all aspects of your business is crucial**, along with a dashboard that lets you see all these metrics in seconds.

## The Framework That Actually Works

After working with dozens of eCommerce businesses in the $1-10M range, the order of priorities is:

1. **Total Contribution Margin**
2. **Total MER**
3. **Segmentation between new and recurring customers**
4. **Incremental ROAS** (operational optimization by channel)
