# Your ROAS Doesn't Mean Shit

**By Juan Cruz Giusto | June 23, 2025**

## Overview

The article argues that Return on Ad Spend (ROAS) is an unreliable metric for eCommerce businesses, presenting three primary concerns about its validity and usefulness.

## Three Core Problems with ROAS

### 1. ROAS Is Manipulated and Unreliable

Advertising platforms inflate reported ROAS to encourage higher spending. Meta uses default attribution windows of "Click in 7 Days / 1 Day View" without encouraging merchants to exclude recent buyers. Google applies a "Standard attribution window of 30 days post-click," sometimes attributing delayed purchases to paid channels.

Without incrementality testing, businesses may overestimate ROI by 150-300%. The article recommends:

- For budgets exceeding $10K/month: Implement incrementality tests
- For smaller budgets: Exclude recent buyers, use tighter attribution windows (7 or 1-day click), employ top-of-funnel creatives

### 2. ROAS Ignores Business Economics

A practical example demonstrates the gap between reported and actual margins:

A 3.0 ROAS ($33 spent per $100 revenue) masks actual economics:
- Revenue: $100
- Acquisition cost: -$33
- VAT (10%): -$10
- Product cost (30%): -$30
- Shipping (5%): -$5
- Returns (10%): -$10
- Payment processing (5%): -$5
- **Real margin: $7** (equivalent to 1.33 actual ROAS)

Fixed costs further erode profitability.

### 3. ROAS Lacks Holistic Context

Optimizing ROAS in isolation ignores interdependent metrics. The author recommends tracking:

**Key Metrics:**
- Paid MER (total platform revenue divided by advertising investment)
- aMER (new customer revenue divided by advertising investment)
- Total MER (complete revenue divided by advertising investment)
- Revenue by new vs. recurring customers
- Customer LTV at 30/60/90/360 days
- Marginal contribution metrics

The framework prioritizes: Total Marginal Contribution -> Total MER -> Customer segmentation -> Incremental ROAS.

Example: If new customer LTV increases 70% by day 90, accepting breakeven ROAS becomes strategically sound -- invisible when examining ROAS alone.

## Conclusion

Profitable eCommerce growth requires measuring incrementality, variable costs, LTV, and comprehensive business metrics rather than relying on potentially inflated ROAS figures.
