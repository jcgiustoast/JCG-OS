# A New Way to Think About LTV in eCommerce

**By Juan Cruz Giusto**
June 25, 2025

---

## The Problem with Our Current Understanding of LTV

The business world is full of rules of thumb or heuristics. Some turn out to be useful, but others are so generic they become misleading.

One of these is the one surrounding LTV: "If your LTV to CAC ratio is greater than 3, you're on the right track." This metric works for venture-backed SaaS companies, which have minimal variable costs and don't need to recover investments immediately.

However, most eCommerce businesses face different realities:

* Many are self-funded and can't wait a year to recover acquisition costs
* eCommerce requires money to produce, store, and ship products
* Following the 3:1 rule can lead to negative cash flow and bankruptcy

**Problematic example:** A brand that acquires customers for $15, has a first-order AOV of $20 ($10 to produce), and an LTV of $45 that materializes over a year faces serious risks without available cash or clear visibility into their situation.

---

## A New Way to Think About LTV

A new paradigm should consider:

* Variable costs
* Specific time periods
* Practical applicability

### Considering Variable Costs

SaaS and eCommerce are fundamentally different businesses. While SaaS has variable costs close to zero (aside from marketing), eCommerce requires production, shipping, and storage.

Therefore, we propose distinguishing between:

**Revenue:** total amount earned

**Value:** Revenue - Variable Costs (Cost of Goods Sold + Shipping)

Under this new definition, LTV represents the money that remains after considering variable costs.

Example: If a customer generates $100 in annual revenue with 50% in variable costs, their LTV is $50.

### Considering Time Periods

One year can be too long for many brands. The solution is to add specific time components to the metric:

* **60-Day LTV:** profit produced 60 days after acquisition
* **120-Day LTV:** profit produced 120 days after acquisition
* **1-Year LTV:** profit produced 365 days after acquisition
* **1+ Year LTV:** profit produced 2/3 years after acquisition

This approach enables differentiated measurement and strategic improvement.

---

## Benefits of This New Paradigm

This conceptual shift provides significant advantages:

* **Practicality:** You can run tests to measure increases in 60-day LTV without relying on proxy metrics like email open rates or NPS

* **Flexibility:** If 60 days seems short, you can track 90-day, 120-day, or 169-day LTV according to specific needs

* **Proximity to cash:** By subtracting variable costs, you can clearly see whether you're making money. The closer the metric is to "cash in the bank," the better your business decisions will be

---

## 5 Ways to Improve LTV

### 1. Offer Subscriptions

Considered the "holy grail" of LTV improvement. If your product is recurring in nature, a subscription option is essential to secure repeat purchases. It must be implemented carefully so as not to put excessive pressure on visitors.

### 2. Optimize Post-Purchase Flows

The month in which customers buy most after their first order is Month 0 (the same month as the purchase). However, many eCommerce brands lack powerful post-purchase flows via email and SMS. This is a powerful tool in the growth arsenal.

### 3. Upselling and Cross-Selling

This doesn't just mean recommended products below the fold on the product page. After purchase, customers interact through: Thank You Page, Account Page, Order Tracking. These spaces should be leveraged to sell complementary products based on previous purchases.

### 4. Create New Flows

Identify the average time it takes for buyers to return and create timely sequences. If you detect that a percentage makes a second purchase 75 days after the first, you should design flows that rekindle the love affair with the brand.

### 5. Introduce New Forms of Communication

Email isn't the only tool. Experiment with SMS, push notifications, Facebook communities, apps. Ideally, measure incremental impact, not just attributed revenue, since costs could outweigh benefits.

### Additional Considerations

* **Improve the unboxing experience:** The customer's first real interaction. A good experience improves brand perception and increases retention rates

* **Develop membership or affiliate programs:** Excellent tactics for keeping customers coming back. Multiple apps can facilitate implementation

---

## Conclusion

This presents a refined paradigm for thinking about LTV that considers eCommerce realities.

**Critical recommendation:** "If you have sufficient volume, experiment with every change" you introduce. A/B Testing is an incredible tool for understanding impact and keeping only positive results. Without adequate volume, there's a risk of implementing strategies that damage long-term results.
