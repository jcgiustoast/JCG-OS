# Why Your eCommerce Has Stopped Growing

**By Juan Cruz Giusto**
October 10, 2025

## The Client Who Grew Until They Stopped

A client came in celebrating having increased their contribution margin by 30% after cutting ad spend in half. However, six months later their revenue had dropped 20%, and they could not understand the cause.

What happened is they were operating on a $20M/year base, dedicating nearly 40% to advertising. When the CFO reframed spend as a cost center instead of a growth lever, they cut 50% of ad investment. They focused on optimizing retention through CRO, email, and SMS marketing.

The first months seemed successful, but gradually targets became unattainable, no matter how many new automations they implemented.

## The Tank Analogy in eCommerce

The business consists of two components:

- **New Customers**: depend on ad investment and organic audience
- **Recurring Customers**: predictable and stable

Recurring customers function like a car's fuel tank. For long trips (medium and long-term growth) we need to "refuel" regularly through acquisition investment.

If we floor the accelerator without refueling (reducing advertising while squeezing existing customers), the tank empties faster and eventually the business stalls.

## The Solution Is Not That Easy

Increasing contribution margin in the short term seems simple: "reduce ad spend and squeeze recurring customers" through intensive promotions.

But this generates consequences. Current recurring customers exist thanks to past ad investment. If present investment is reduced, recurring customers will be missing in the future.

When you try to recover by drastically increasing ad spend, acquisition costs rise so much that you lose profitability. The eCommerce cannot attract that volume without compromising margins.

The only solution is to "lower growth expectations, increase acquisition budget, and progressively rebuild that recurring customer base."

## Not All Recurring Customers Are Equal

The common mistake is assuming all recurring customers have equal value. A customer who bought 2 months ago has a different repurchase probability than one from 12 months ago.

Cohort reports reveal that retention is highest immediately after the first purchase, decreasing gradually. Additionally, making a second purchase significantly increases the probability of a third.

Segmenting by time since last purchase allows you to predict recurring customer revenue.

## The 3 Types of Recurring Customers

ASTEROI segments recurring customers into:

- **Active Recurring**: purchased recently
- **At-Risk Recurring**: purchased some time ago but are not considered lost
- **Dormant Recurring**: have not purchased in a long time, unlikely to return

The time cutoffs vary by industry. A jewelry customer with no purchases in 100 days may be "Active," while in supplements they would be "Lost."

## Awake Customers: The True Fuel

The real capacity to generate revenue is not just the total number of recurring customers (which always increases), but "how many are truly awake and ready to buy."

**Formula:**

Awake Customers = New Customers Acquired + Active Recurring + At-Risk Recurring - Dormant Recurring

This metric indicates customers with a high repurchase probability and the capacity to generate recurring revenue. It allows you to see monthly evolution and make informed strategic decisions.

If you want growth with recurring contribution, you must invest in advertising to increase Awake Customers. If you optimize margins by cutting marketing, you will damage future capacity and must lower expectations.

## The End of the Story

The initial client had already spent months with reduced ad spend when they hired ASTEROI. They were 15-20% below target.

The diagnosis was clear: they had cut Awake Customers in half without being able to increase advertising without losing profitability.

After explaining the situation to the CMO and CFO, both understood that "optimizing short-term profit comes at the cost of long-term growth."

A realistic forecast was created with gradual increases in ad spend while protecting profitability. A commercial calendar was developed for short-term cash needs, and dozens of ads were created for Meta, Google, and TikTok.

In 6 months they met their targets and were on track to exceed previous projections.

## The Lesson

eCommerce strategy must integrate short-term and long-term perspectives. Otherwise, there is a risk of losing market share, reducing revenue, and being unable to cover costs.
