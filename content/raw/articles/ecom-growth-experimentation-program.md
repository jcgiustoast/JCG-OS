# 10 Things You Need to Run a Successful eCom Growth & Experimentation Program

**Author:** JC Giusto
**Published:** October 8, 2024

## Introduction

Launching an eCommerce Growth and Experimentation Program presents significant challenges. While it can substantially impact your business, several pitfalls can derail progress:

- Making decisions and creating experiments without strategic direction
- Failing to uncover customer and business insights
- Neglecting research, which forms the foundation of successful programs

Investing thousands of dollars in ineffective areas risks missing bottom-line impact. These 10 elements can boost your program's success rate:

---

## 1. A North Star Metric

An effective growth program requires a North Star Metric -- a guiding compass directing focus toward primary company growth objectives aligned with customer satisfaction.

According to MixPanel, a North Star metric keeps organizations focused on "what makes your customers happiest and keeps them coming back for more."

**Examples:**
- **Airbnb:** Nights Booked
- **TrueClassic:** Customers purchasing more than five t-shirts
- **AG1 (Athletic Greens):** Number achieving fitness goals

**Why not just revenue?** Long-term revenue tells only part of the story. Jim Collins, author of *Built to Last*, notes that "visionary companies pursue a cluster of objectives, of which making money is only one." These companies paradoxically generate more revenue than purely profit-driven competitors.

---

## 2. High-Quality Data

High-quality data requires completeness and trustworthiness.

**Initial tracking** may include visitors and conversion rates. **As programs mature**, capture additional metrics:
- Revenue
- Cost of goods sold
- Customer IDs
- Return rates

This enables you to:
- Identify products with best margins or fewer returns
- Measure results by profit rather than revenue
- Experiment with longer-term metrics (NPS, LTV, churn)

**Trust is critical.** Most decisions depend on data integrity. Ensure events fire correctly, the DataLayer captures complete information, and your team understands the analytics platform. "Discovering errors can erode trust in the results and complicate future efforts."

---

## 3. Research Framework

Testing and implementing strategies without research resembles "throwing spaghetti at the wall and hoping something sticks." Growth strategies and experiments require "in-depth qualitative and quantitative analysis."

**Essential research methods:**
- GA4 data analysis for friction and bottleneck detection
- Heatmaps
- Screen recordings
- Post-purchase surveys
- User testing
- Motivation surveys (understanding purchase drivers)
- Business modeling (identifying highest leverage points)

This approach increases success likelihood and enhances action effectiveness.

---

## 4. Multidisciplinary Team

David Epstein's *Range* demonstrates that labs with diverse scientists produce more breakthroughs. The same applies to growth teams.

**Include members from:**
- SEO
- Email marketing
- Paid ads
- Product & UX
- Engineering
- Data analysis

"Each member brings their unique experience, leading to original, high-potential ideas."

---

## 5. Prioritization Framework

With limited time and resources, a prioritization framework ranks ideas by success likelihood. Popular frameworks include PIE, ICE, and PXL. For beginners, the ICE Score is intuitive:

- **Impact:** Potential uplift from the change or experiment
- **Confidence:** Success likelihood
- **Effort:** Implementation difficulty

---

## 6. Documentation Platform

This represents "the most important element of a Growth Program." As experiments progress, store results on a centralized platform.

Benefits include:
- Team visibility into what worked and what didn't
- Informed future experiments
- Improved decisions by Paid Ads and UX teams based on customer behavior data

---

## 7. Communication Channels

Breakthrough ideas emerge unexpectedly. Jeff Bezos didn't invent Amazon Prime -- a junior software engineer did. However, Bezos "created a bottom-up communication channel that allowed ideas to reach the top."

Build systems to capture and foster ideas organization-wide. Share successful experiments, roadmaps, and lessons to generate excitement about ongoing work and build an experimentation culture.

---

## 8. A/B Testing Tool

Exponential growth emerges when positive effects compound while negative effects are discarded. The challenge: distinguishing experiments producing uplifts from those that don't.

Randomized controlled experiments (A/B tests) isolate single variables, analyzing their impact. "Each positive experiment builds on the previous ones, compounding the effects."

Results don't simply add -- they multiply, making A/B testing invaluable.

---

## 9. Knowledge About Statistics

Statistics expertise is non-negotiable. Without it, "you risk making decisions based on faulty interpretations, potentially losing millions."

Critical concepts include:
- Peeking
- False positives and false negatives
- False discovery rates
- Non-binomial analysis

Ensure your team or hired agency demonstrates statistical proficiency and honesty.

---

## 10. Willingness to Embrace Failure

Growth ideas fail more often than succeed -- and that's normal. The work's nature is inherently uncertain.

Despite thorough GA4 analysis, customer interviews, and detailed prioritization frameworks, experiment success isn't guaranteed. These elements reduce failure risk and strengthen hypotheses but never ensure success.

The solution: accept failure as part of the process. "If our ideas are based on research and creativity, it's only a matter of time before one of them hits a home run."

---

## Conclusion

Growth and Experimentation is challenging. However, neglecting these 10 elements makes it significantly harder. Integration of these components substantially increases program effectiveness and success probability.
